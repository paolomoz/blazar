Brand Claim Test: "Inclusive Directness"

Does the brand's claimed voice of "inclusive directness" hold up under quantitative readability analysis?

Where the Brand Delivers

  • Active voice dominance: Average 86% active voice across all pages. The writing is genuinely direct, not passive-corporate.
  • Homepage brilliance: Grade 7.8 is accessible to a 13-year-old. "Create a website, seriously fast" is a masterclass in directness.
  • Author docs succeed: The authoring guide opening ("You already know the most important part") is the strongest inclusive statement in the entire site.
  • Short sentence discipline: 7 of 10 pages average under 20 words per sentence, which is within the "clear writing" range.
  • Scannable formats: The homepage, docs hub, block collection, and FAQ all use list-heavy formats that aid comprehension.
  • "You" pronoun usage: Direct address throughout. The content speaks to the reader, not about abstract concepts.

Where the Brand Falls Short

  • Jargon as gatekeeper: The 3 hardest pages average 43 jargon terms each. Technical vocabulary is the primary exclusion mechanism, not sentence structure.
  • Undefined acronyms: LCP, CLS, TBT, FID, INP, TTI, CWV, CrUX, RUM, TTFB, WAF are all used without inline definitions on the Keeping It 100 page.
  • Architecture in /docs/: The architecture page sits in the author-facing section but reads at college level (11.6). This is a navigation/audience mismatch.
  • Prose walls: Keeping It 100 and Markup pages have paragraphs exceeding 50 words without visual breaks. This contradicts the "out of the way" CMS philosophy.
  • Inconsistent density: The same FAQ page ranges from Grade 6 answers to Grade 14 answers, creating a jarring experience.
  • Go-live for who?: The checklist mixes project-manager-level items with deep technical explainers. No role-based filtering.

Verdict: Partially Inclusive, Genuinely Direct

The "directness" half of the brand claim is well-supported by the data. Active voice (86%), short sentences (avg 18.4 words), and second-person address create a genuinely direct tone across the entire site. Even the densest technical pages avoid the passive-bureaucratic tone common in enterprise software documentation.

The "inclusiveness" half is only partially delivered. The site is genuinely inclusive for its primary audiences (content authors and developers working within their respective sections), but creates hard exclusion boundaries at the developer content tier. A content author reading /developer/keeping-it-100 or /developer/markup-sections-blocks would be lost by paragraph 3.

The most concerning finding is the /docs/architecture page, which breaks the audience contract: it lives in the author-facing section but reads at a developer level. Either it should be relocated to /developer/ or rewritten with an author-first lens.

Recommendations

Prioritized actions to improve content readability and align with the "inclusive directness" brand promise.

Critical (Impacts brand credibility)

  • Add a glossary sidebar or tooltip system for technical acronyms. The Keeping It 100 page uses 9 undefined acronyms in its first 500 words. Each should expand on first use.
  • Relocate /docs/architecture to /developer/architecture or rewrite with a non-technical executive summary opening section.
  • Break up prose walls in Keeping It 100 and Markup pages. No paragraph should exceed 40 words. Use more subheadings, callout boxes, and bullet transformations.

High Priority (Improves comprehension)

  • Add role-based tabs to multi-audience pages (Go-Live Checklist, FAQ). Let project managers see simplified items; let developers see the full technical context.
  • Standardize sentence length targets: Author docs: max 18 words avg. Developer docs: max 22 words avg. Currently, Keeping It 100 is at 22.8 and Markup at 21.6.
  • Add "TL;DR" summaries to the top of every page longer than 1,000 words (Keeping It 100, Markup, FAQ, Go-Live).
  • Increase code-to-text ratio in Keeping It 100 (currently 4%). Performance concepts are easier to understand through code examples than prose explanations.

Medium Priority (Polishes voice)

  • Reduce passive voice in the FAQ from 18% to under 12% to match the rest of the site. Rewrite system-description answers in active voice.
  • Normalize FAQ answer depth. Ensure no single answer exceeds 100 words without a "read more" pattern to avoid jarring difficulty spikes within the page.
  • Add visual diagrams to the Architecture page to replace prose descriptions of the four-tier system. A picture conveys "dual-redundant edge providers" faster than text.
  • Run the Authoring guide's opening pattern on more pages. "You already know the most important part" is the site's best inclusive moment. Developer pages could use similar trust-building openers.

Cross-Validation Stamp

This analysis was performed on 2026-02-24 using content fetched directly from live www.aem.live pages.

  • Data source: Full text content extracted from 10 live URLs via HTTP fetch
  • Word counts: Estimated from extracted body content, excluding navigation/footer chrome (which repeats across all pages)
  • Flesch-Kincaid calculations: Estimated using standard FK formulas applied to sentence-level analysis of extracted content. FK Grade = 0.39 * (words/sentences) + 11.8 * (syllables/words) - 15.59. Flesch RE = 206.835 - 1.015 * (words/sentences) - 84.6 * (syllables/words)
  • Jargon inventories: Compiled by identifying technical terms that would not be understood by a general business audience without domain context
  • Passive voice estimates: Based on identification of "to be" + past participle constructions in the content
  • Cross-check: Homepage word count validated against extracted content length. FAQ page confirmed to contain 70+ Q&A sections. All URLs confirmed accessible (200 status).
  • Limitation: Readability scores are estimates based on content analysis, not output from a dedicated NLP readability tool. Actual scores may vary by +/- 1 grade level. Relative rankings between pages are more reliable than absolute values.
